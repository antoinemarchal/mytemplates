About



Our understanding of the star formation in galaxies is highly constraint by our knowledge of the physical processes which govern the interstellar gas. Stars form by gravitational instabilities and turbulent compression (see Elmegreen, B. G. 2004 for a review) in very dense and cold (T~10 K) molecular clouds whose formation is strongly constraint by the structure of the cold clouds of neutral hydrogen (HI). The current vision of the neutral ISM comes from an important legacy. Field, G., Goldsmith, D., & Habing, H. 1969 has postulated a two phase model to describe the neutral hydrogen in the ISM : A cold (T~50K, n~50 cm-3) dense phase, the cold neutral medium (CNM) and a diffuse warm (T~8000K, n~0.1 cm-3) phase, the warm neutral medium (WNM). A more complex description of the ISM, including a warm ionized medium (WIM) and a hot ionized medium (HIM), has been established by McKee, C. F. & Ostriker, J. P. 1977 . Considering heating (dominated by photo-electric effect on small dust grains) and cooling (dominated by CII-158 micrometer, OI-63 micrometer, Lyman-alpha and electron recombinations onto positive charged grains) processes of the ISM, it is commonly accepted that the cold and warm phases can coexist being in two thermally stable phases in pressure equilibrium over a range of pressures Pmin < P < Pmax ( Wolfire, M. G., McKee, C. F., Hollenbach, D., & Tielens, A. 2003 ).

The most commoly used technique to extract information about the cold and warm phases in emission is the Gaussian decomposition. The result presented here is performed on HI4PI (a full sky survey of the 21 cm line) with a code developped at the Institut d'Astrophysique Spatiale (IAS) which take into consideration a spatial coherence of the field to find a solution.
